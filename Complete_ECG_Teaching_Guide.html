SINUS RHYTHMS - Origin: SA Node
Normal Sinus Rhythm (NSR)
Origin: Sinoatrial (SA) Node | Rate: 60-100 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: 60-100 beats/min
Rhythm: Regular (R-R intervals consistent)
P Waves: Upright in Lead II, uniform, one before each QRS
PR Interval: 0.12-0.20 seconds (3-5 small boxes)
QRS Complex: Narrow, <0.12 seconds
P:QRS Ratio: 1:1

Key Teaching Points

For a rhythm to be classified as SINUS, TWO criteria must be met: (1) A 1:1 relationship between P waves and QRS complexes, and (2) P wave morphology indicating SA node origin (upright in Lead II, inverted in aVR).

Sinus Bradycardia
Origin: SA Node | Rate: <60 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: <60 beats/min
Rhythm: Regular
P Waves: Upright, uniform, precede each QRS
PR Interval: 0.12-0.20 seconds (normal)
QRS Complex: Narrow, <0.12 seconds
P:QRS Ratio: 1:1

Etiology

  • Increased vagal tone
  • Intrinsic SA node disease
  • Hypothermia
  • Hypoxia
  • Hypothyroidism
  • Beta blockers, Ca channel blockers, digitalis
  • Inferior wall MI
  • Athletic conditioning (normal)

Management

Asymptomatic: Observation only
Symptomatic (hypotension, AMS, chest pain, CHF): Atropine 1mg IV q3-5min (max 3mg) ‚Üí TCP ‚Üí Dopamine/Epinephrine drip

Sinus Tachycardia
Origin: SA Node | Rate: >100 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: >100 bpm (usually 100-160)
Rhythm: Regular
P Waves: Upright, uniform, 1:1 (may merge with T at high rates)
PR Interval: 0.12-0.20 seconds
QRS Complex: Narrow
P:QRS Ratio: 1:1

Etiology

  • Exercise
  • Fever / Infection / Sepsis
  • Pain / Anxiety
  • Hypovolemia
  • Hyperthyroidism
  • Anemia
  • Heart failure
  • Pulmonary embolism
  • Sympathomimetic drugs
  • Caffeine / Alcohol

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical Management Point

Sinus tachycardia is usually a COMPENSATORY response. TREAT THE UNDERLYING CAUSE, NOT THE RATE!
Treating the rate without addressing the cause can lead to cardiovascular collapse!
Sinus Arrhythmia
Origin: SA Node | Rate varies with respiration

ECG Characteristics

Rate: Usually 60-100 bpm (varies)
Rhythm: Irregular - P-P varies >0.16 sec
P Waves: Upright, uniform morphology
PR Interval: Constant
QRS Complex: Narrow
Pattern: Rate ‚Üë inspiration, ‚Üì expiration

Management

No treatment required. This is a benign, normal variant - especially common in young, healthy patients.

ATRIAL RHYTHMS - Origin: Atrial Tissue
Atrial Flutter
Origin: Rapid Atrial Reentry Circuit | Atrial Rate: 250-350/min

ECG Characteristics

Atrial Rate: 250-350/min (~300 typical)
Ventricular Rate: Depends on block (2:1=150, 4:1=75)
Rhythm: Usually regular if fixed block ratio
P Waves: Absent - SAWTOOTH flutter waves (F waves)
QRS Complex: Usually narrow
Key Feature: Classic sawtooth baseline

Management

Stable: Rate control with diltiazem or beta blockers
Unstable: Synchronized cardioversion
CAUTION: Avoid cardioversion in new-onset without anticoagulation (stroke risk)

Atrial Fibrillation (A-Fib)
Origin: Multiple Atrial Foci | Atrial Rate: 350-600/min

ECG Characteristics

Atrial Rate: 350-600/min (fibrillatory)
Ventricular Rate: Variable (RVR >100, controlled 60-100)
Rhythm: IRREGULARLY IRREGULAR
P Waves: Absent - chaotic f waves
QRS Complex: Usually narrow
Baseline: Chaotic, undulating

Clinical Significance

Loss of atrial kick reduces cardiac output by ~15%. "Holiday heart" = paroxysmal A-fib after heavy alcohol.

Management

Stable: Rate control with diltiazem or beta blockers
Unstable: Synchronized cardioversion
Long-term: Anticoagulation for stroke prevention

Wandering Atrial Pacemaker (WAP)
Origin: Multiple Atrial Sites | Rate: Usually 60-100 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: Usually 60-100 bpm
Rhythm: Slightly irregular
P Waves: At least 3 DIFFERENT morphologies
PR Interval: Variable (changes with pacemaker site)
QRS Complex: Narrow
Key Feature: Changing P wave shapes

Management

Usually benign - no treatment required. Often seen in healthy individuals, especially athletes and elderly.

Multifocal Atrial Tachycardia (MAT)
Origin: Multiple Atrial Sites | Rate: >100 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: >100 bpm (often 100-180)
Rhythm: Irregularly irregular
P Waves: At least 3 different morphologies
PR Interval: Variable
QRS Complex: Narrow
vs WAP: Same appearance but rate >100

Etiology

Commonly associated with COPD and respiratory failure. Also seen with: CHF, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, theophylline toxicity.

Management

Treat underlying cause (usually pulmonary disease). Magnesium sulfate may help. DO NOT cardiovert - will not work.

JUNCTIONAL RHYTHMS - Origin: AV Junction
Junctional Escape Rhythm
Origin: AV Junction | Intrinsic Rate: 40-60 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: 40-60 bpm (intrinsic junctional rate)
Rhythm: Regular
P Waves: Absent, inverted after QRS, or hidden in QRS
PR Interval: Short (<0.12 sec) if P before QRS
QRS Complex: Narrow (<0.12 sec)
Key Feature: AV junction takes over as pacemaker

Etiology

  • SA node failure
  • Increased vagal tone
  • Digitalis toxicity
  • Beta blocker/Ca channel blocker
  • Inferior wall MI
  • Hypoxia
  • Post cardiac surgery

Management

Asymptomatic: Monitor
Symptomatic: Atropine 0.5-1mg IV ‚Üí TCP ‚Üí Dopamine/Epinephrine drip

Accelerated Junctional Rhythm
Origin: AV Junction | Rate: 60-100 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: 60-100 bpm
Rhythm: Regular
P Waves: Absent, inverted, or retrograde
QRS Complex: Narrow
Key Feature: Junctional but enhanced automaticity
Common Cause: Digitalis toxicity

Management

Usually well-tolerated - treat underlying cause. If digitalis toxicity suspected, hold digoxin and check levels.

Junctional Tachycardia
Origin: AV Junction | Rate: >100 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: >100 bpm (often 100-180)
Rhythm: Regular
P Waves: Absent, inverted, or retrograde
QRS Complex: Narrow
vs SVT: May be indistinguishable
Common Cause: Digitalis toxicity, MI

Management

Stable: Vagal maneuvers, adenosine (may help diagnose)
Unstable: Synchronized cardioversion
Note: Often treated same as SVT if origin unclear

ECTOPIC BEATS - Premature Complexes
Premature Atrial Complex (PAC)
Origin: Ectopic Atrial Focus

ECG Characteristics

Timing: Occurs EARLY (before expected beat)
P Wave: Different morphology from sinus P waves
QRS Complex: Usually narrow (same as sinus)
Pause: Incomplete compensatory pause
PR Interval: May be normal, short, or prolonged
Significance: Usually benign if isolated

Management

Isolated PACs: Observation only - usually benign
Frequent PACs: May trigger A-fib, A-flutter, or SVT - monitor closely

Premature Junctional Complex (PJC)
Origin: AV Junction

ECG Characteristics

Timing: Occurs EARLY
P Wave: Absent, inverted after QRS, or hidden
QRS Complex: Narrow (same as sinus)
PR Interval: Short (<0.12 sec) if P before QRS
Pause: May have compensatory pause
Key Feature: Junctional appearance but premature

Management

Occasional PJCs: Usually not significant - observe
Frequent PJCs: May indicate digitalis toxicity, hypoxia, or heart disease

Premature Ventricular Complex (PVC)
Origin: Ventricular Ectopic Focus

ECG Characteristics

Timing: Occurs EARLY
P Wave: Usually absent (may see retrograde)
QRS Complex: WIDE (>0.12 sec), bizarre morphology
T Wave: Opposite direction from QRS
Pause: FULL compensatory pause
Patterns: Unifocal, multifocal, couplets, bigeminy, trigeminy

PVC Patterns

Unifocal: Same shape (same focus) | Multifocal: Different shapes (different foci - more concerning)
Bigeminy: Every other beat is PVC | Trigeminy: Every 3rd beat is PVC
Couplet: 2 PVCs in a row | R-on-T: PVC falls on T wave (dangerous!)

Management

Isolated/unifocal: Usually benign - observe, treat underlying cause
Frequent/multifocal/R-on-T: Oxygen, consider Amiodarone or Lidocaine
With hemodynamic instability: Treat as per ACLS algorithms

VENTRICULAR RHYTHMS - Origin: Ventricles
Idioventricular (Ventricular Escape) Rhythm
Origin: Ventricles | Intrinsic Rate: 20-40 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: 20-40 bpm (intrinsic ventricular rate)
Rhythm: Usually regular
P Waves: Absent (or dissociated)
QRS Complex: WIDE (>0.12 sec)
Significance: "Escape" rhythm - ventricles take over
Prognosis: Often a dying heart rhythm

‚ö†Ô∏è Management

This is a "last resort" pacemaker. DO NOT suppress with antiarrhythmics - you'll cause asystole!
Treatment: Atropine (limited effect), TCP, Dopamine/Epinephrine, treat underlying cause
Accelerated Idioventricular Rhythm (AIVR)
Origin: Ventricles | Rate: 40-100 bpm

ECG Characteristics

Rate: 40-100 bpm
Rhythm: Usually regular
P Waves: Absent or dissociated
QRS Complex: Wide (>0.12 sec)
Common Cause: Reperfusion after MI (good sign!)
Also seen with: Digitalis toxicity, electrolyte imbalance

Management

Usually benign and self-limiting. Often a sign of successful reperfusion after thrombolytics or PCI.
DO NOT treat with antiarrhythmics unless hemodynamically unstable. Observe and monitor.

Ventricular Tachycardia (V-Tach)
Origin: Ventricles | Rate: >100 bpm (usually 150-250)

ECG Characteristics

Rate: >100 bpm (usually 150-250)
Rhythm: Usually regular
P Waves: Usually absent (or AV dissociation)
QRS Complex: WIDE (>0.12 sec), bizarre
Types: Monomorphic (same shape) or Polymorphic
3+ PVCs in a row: = V-Tach by definition

‚ö†Ô∏è Management - LIFE THREATENING

WITH PULSE - Stable: Amiodarone 150mg IV over 10 min, may repeat; or Lidocaine
WITH PULSE - Unstable: Synchronized cardioversion 100J ‚Üí 200J ‚Üí 300J ‚Üí 360J
PULSELESS: Defibrillate immediately! CPR + ACLS cardiac arrest algorithm

Ventricular Fibrillation (V-Fib)
Origin: Multiple Ventricular Foci | PULSELESS ARREST RHYTHM

ECG Characteristics

Rate: Cannot be determined
Rhythm: Chaotic, disorganized
P Waves: None
QRS Complex: None - chaotic fibrillatory waves
Types: Coarse (large waves) vs Fine (small waves)
Pulse: NONE - Cardiac arrest!

‚ö†Ô∏è IMMEDIATE TREATMENT REQUIRED

1. Confirm pulseless
2. Begin CPR immediately
3. DEFIBRILLATE as soon as possible - 200J biphasic (or 360J monophasic)
4. Resume CPR for 2 minutes
5. Epinephrine 1mg IV/IO q3-5min
6. Consider Amiodarone 300mg IV/IO (then 150mg)
7. Treat reversible causes (H's and T's)
Torsades de Pointes
Polymorphic V-Tach with QT prolongation | "Twisting of the points"

ECG Characteristics

Rate: 150-250 bpm
Rhythm: Irregular
QRS: Wide, changes amplitude - "twists" around baseline
Associated with: Prolonged QT interval
Causes: Drugs, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia
Danger: Can degenerate to V-Fib

‚ö†Ô∏è Management

PULSELESS: Defibrillation + CPR
WITH PULSE: MAGNESIUM SULFATE 1-2g IV (first-line!)
Correct electrolytes (K+, Mg++), stop offending drugs, consider overdrive pacing

AV BLOCKS - Conduction Disturbances
First-Degree AV Block
Delayed conduction through AV node | PR >0.20 sec

ECG Characteristics

Rate: Usually normal (depends on underlying rhythm)
Rhythm: Regular
P Waves: Normal, upright, 1:1 with QRS
PR Interval: PROLONGED >0.20 sec (constant)
QRS Complex: Usually narrow
Key Point: Every P wave conducts - just delayed

Management

Usually benign - no treatment required. Monitor for progression to higher-degree blocks.
Common causes: Increased vagal tone, AV nodal blocking drugs, inferior MI, aging

Second-Degree AV Block Type I (Wenckebach/Mobitz I)
Progressive PR prolongation until dropped beat

ECG Characteristics

Rate: Atrial regular, ventricular irregular
Rhythm: Irregular (grouped beating)
P Waves: More P waves than QRS complexes
PR Interval: PROGRESSIVELY LENGTHENS until beat dropped
QRS Complex: Usually narrow
Pattern: "Longer, longer, longer, drop!"

Management

Usually benign - often transient and asymptomatic. Monitor closely.
If symptomatic: Atropine may help (block is usually at AV node level)
Common causes: Inferior MI, increased vagal tone, AV nodal blocking drugs

Second-Degree AV Block Type II (Mobitz II)
Sudden dropped beats WITHOUT PR prolongation

ECG Characteristics

Rate: Atrial regular, ventricular may be irregular
Rhythm: Irregular when beats drop
P Waves: More P waves than QRS
PR Interval: CONSTANT (doesn't lengthen before drop)
QRS Complex: Often WIDE (infranodal block)
vs Type I: No warning - sudden drop

‚ö†Ô∏è Management - MORE SERIOUS than Type I

Can progress suddenly to complete heart block!
Atropine often INEFFECTIVE (block below AV node)
TCP should be readily available
Prepare for possible permanent pacemaker
Third-Degree (Complete) AV Block
Complete AV dissociation - NO conduction from atria to ventricles

ECG Characteristics

Atrial Rate: Usually 60-100 (sinus)
Ventricular Rate: 20-60 (escape rhythm)
P Waves: Present, regular, but NO relationship to QRS
PR Interval: Completely VARIABLE (no pattern)
QRS Complex: Narrow if junctional escape, WIDE if ventricular
Key Finding: AV DISSOCIATION - P's march through QRS's

‚ö†Ô∏è Management - SERIOUS EMERGENCY

Prepare TCP immediately!
Atropine may be tried but often ineffective (especially with wide QRS)
Dopamine/Epinephrine infusion as bridge
Patient will need permanent pacemaker
DO NOT give AV nodal blockers!
ARREST RHYTHMS - Cardiac Arrest
Asystole
NO electrical activity | "Flatline" | NON-SHOCKABLE

ECG Characteristics

Rate: None (0)
Rhythm: None
P Waves: Absent
QRS Complex: Absent
Must Rule Out: Loose lead, fine V-fib, lead in wrong position
Prognosis: Very poor survival rate

‚ö†Ô∏è Management - NON-SHOCKABLE RHYTHM

1. Confirm asystole in 2 leads
2. High-quality CPR - minimize interruptions
3. Epinephrine 1mg IV/IO q3-5min
4. Consider reversible causes (H's and T's)
5. DO NOT DEFIBRILLATE - no shockable rhythm
Consider termination criteria if prolonged resuscitation unsuccessful
Pulseless Electrical Activity (PEA)
Organized electrical activity WITHOUT detectable pulse | NON-SHOCKABLE

ECG Characteristics

ECG Appearance: Can look like ANY organized rhythm
Pulse: ABSENT - this defines PEA
Common Causes: H's and T's (see below)
Key Point: Electromechanical dissociation
Prognosis: Better than asystole if cause found
Critical: Must identify and treat cause!

H's and T's - Reversible Causes

H's:
‚Ä¢ Hypovolemia
‚Ä¢ Hypoxia
‚Ä¢ Hydrogen ion (acidosis)
‚Ä¢ Hypo/Hyperkalemia
‚Ä¢ Hypothermia
‚Ä¢ Hypoglycemia
T's:
‚Ä¢ Tension pneumothorax
‚Ä¢ Tamponade (cardiac)
‚Ä¢ Toxins (overdose)
‚Ä¢ Thrombosis (MI)
‚Ä¢ Thrombosis (PE)

‚ö†Ô∏è Management - NON-SHOCKABLE RHYTHM

1. High-quality CPR
2. Epinephrine 1mg IV/IO q3-5min
3. AGGRESSIVELY search for and treat reversible causes (H's and T's)
4. DO NOT DEFIBRILLATE - organized rhythm, just no mechanical output
Consider: IV fluids, needle decompression, FAST exam if available
