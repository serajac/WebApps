ECG Waveforms & Fundamentals

Every bump, wave, and segment tells a specific physiological story. Master each component first.

Annotated Normal Beat — Lead II
THE DOMINO EFFECT

SA node tips the first domino (P wave) → AV node tollbooth pauses it (PR) → roars down both bundle branches (QRS = coordinated contraction) → T wave resets the dominoes. One beat, one complete cycle.

P
P Wave
Lead II
  • Represents: Atrial depolarization
  • Normal: <2.5mm, <0.12 sec
  • Upright in II = sinus rhythm confirmed
  • Negative in aVR always
QRS
QRS Complex
Lead II
  • Represents: Ventricular depolarization
  • Normal: <0.11 sec (narrow)
  • Wide (>0.11): BBB or aberrant conduction
  • Q wave: First negative deflection
ST/T
ST Segment & T Wave
Lead II
  • ST segment: Normally isoelectric (flat)
  • T wave: Ventricular repolarization
  • T direction: Same as dominant QRS
  • J point: Junction of QRS and ST
PR
PR Interval — The AV Node Tollbooth
PR Interval Highlighted
HIGHWAY TOLLBOOTH

Normal PR (0.12–0.20s) = smooth traffic. PR >0.20s = 1st degree block (backed-up). PR <0.12s = WPW bypass tract (jumped the toll lane).

DurationMeaning
<0.12 secWPW or junctional — pre-excitation
0.12–0.20Normal ✓
>0.20 sec1st Degree AV Block
QT INTERVALNormal = <½ of R-R interval. Prolonged QT → Torsades risk (R-on-T phenomenon). Drugs: quinidine, TCAs, phenothiazines. Electrolytes: hypokalemia, hypocalcemia.

◈ Module 02

The 12-Lead System

12 unique camera angles on the same electrical event. Together they provide complete 3D spatial coverage of the heart.

12 PHOTOGRAPHERS, ONE SUBJECT

Imagine 12 photographers surrounding a building (the heart) from different positions. No single photo tells the whole story — together they give a complete picture. Each lead is a unique camera angle on cardiac electrical activity.

I–III
Standard Limb Leads

Bipolar, frontal plane. Measure electrical difference between two limb electrodes.

  • Lead I: RA(−)→LA(+) — lateral left
  • Lead II: RA(−)→LL(+) — inferior, best P-wave lead
  • Lead III: LA(−)→LL(+) — inferior, right-leaning
LEAD REVERSAL CHECKaVR must ALWAYS be globally negative. If positive → suspect RA/LA lead reversal!
aVR/L/F
Augmented Limb Leads

Unipolar, frontal plane. Machine amplifies ("augments") weak signal ~50%.

  • aVR: Right shoulder — cavity lead, globally negative
  • aVL: Left shoulder — lateral/superior view
  • aVF: Left foot — inferior view toward diaphragm
V1–V6
Precordial Chest Leads

Six unipolar leads, horizontal plane. Wrap chest left-to-right across the transverse axis.

  • V1: Right sternal border, 4th ICS
  • V2: Left sternal border, 4th ICS
  • V3–V4: Anterior wall territory (LAD)
  • V5–V6: Lateral wall territory (circumflex)

◈ Module 03 + 04

Bundle Branch Blocks

When the QRS widens beyond 0.11 sec, the electrical highway has a roadblock. Master the 3-lead method in seconds.

THE ELECTRICAL HIGHWAY

Bundle branches are major interstates delivering signals to both ventricles simultaneously. When a highway closes, traffic takes back-roads through muscle — slower, causing wide slurred QRS. The RIGHT bundle is a long winding road, prone to disruption. The LEFT bundle has two exits (fascicles): anterior and posterior.

1

Find the Widest QRS Across All 12 Leads

Clearly >0.11s (more than half a large box at standard speed) = abnormal conduction present.

2

Examine Dr. Grauer's 3 KEY Leads: I, V1, V6

These three leads ALONE differentiate RBBB from LBBB from IVCD. No other leads needed for classification.

3

Apply the Pattern

RBBB: rSR' in V1 + wide S in I and V6. LBBB: QS in V1 + broad notched R in I and V6. Neither = IVCD.

RIGHT Bundle Branch Block (RBBB) — "Turn Signal Right →"
Lead V1 — rSR' "Rabbit Ears" (R' taller than r)
Lead V6 — Wide Slurred Terminal S Wave
Lead I — Wide Slurred Terminal S Wave
LEFT Bundle Branch Block (LBBB) — "Turn Signal Left ←"
Lead V1 — QS Pattern (No initial r, broad negative)
Lead V6 — Broad Notched R, Inverted T
Lead I — Broad Monophasic R, Inverted T
R
RBBB — Physiology Explained
WHY rSR' IN V1?

① LV depolarizes first → small r in V1. ② LV forces go LEFT (away from V1) → S dip. ③ RV depolarizes LATE through myocardium going RIGHT → taller R' in V1. The second ear (R') is ALWAYS taller than first (r).

  • QRS ≥0.12 sec (complete RBBB)
  • V1: rSR' — "rabbit ears" / M-shaped
  • I, V6: Wide terminal S wave (slurred)
  • ST-T: Opposite direction to terminal QRS
CLINICAL SIGNIFICANCERBBB alone often benign. Critical when: new RBBB + AMI, bifascicular block (RBBB + hemiblock), or high-degree AV block → prepare TCP.
L
LBBB — Physiology Explained
WHY QS IN V1?

Normally septum depolarizes left-to-right giving small r in V1. In LBBB the right bundle fires first, REVERSING septal depolarization right-to-left → tiny r disappears → V1 sees only forces going AWAY = deep QS pattern.

  • QRS ≥0.12 sec
  • V1–V2: QS or rS pattern (absent or minimal r)
  • I, V5, V6: Broad monophasic R, often notched
  • I, V6: NO Q waves (septal Q absent)
⚠ CRITICALLBBB ALWAYS indicates underlying heart disease. New LBBB + chest pain = STEMI equivalent (activate cath lab). Cannot diagnose LVH or Q-wave MI in presence of LBBB.

◈ Module 05

Ischemia, Injury & Infarction

Three stages of myocardial oxygen deprivation — each with distinct ECG signatures. Time-to-recognition directly saves muscle.

THE CAMPFIRE DANGER ZONES

Ischemia = You feel the heat but step back — reversible (T wave changes, ST depression). Injury/STEMI = You're getting burned but not yet scarred — still reversible with RAPID intervention (ST elevation). Infarction = Deep permanent burn — irreversible (Q waves). Act at the INJURY stage — every minute = 1.9 million cardiomyocytes lost.

≈
ISCHEMIA
ST Depression + T Inversion
  • Horizontal or downsloping ST depression ≥0.5mm
  • Symmetrical T wave inversion
  • Normal QRS width (narrow complex)
  • Reversible if perfusion restored
KEY SIGNDownsloping ST depression is more specific for ischemia than horizontal. Deeper depression = more severe ischemia.
↑
INJURY (STEMI)
Convex ST Elevation — "Tombstone"
  • Convex (domed/coved) ST elevation upward
  • ≥1mm in ≥2 contiguous limb leads
  • ≥2mm in ≥2 contiguous precordial leads
  • J point elevation — measure 0.04–0.08s out
⚠ STEMI = ACT NOWReversible — activate cath lab immediately. Door-to-balloon <90 min goal.
Q
INFARCTION
Pathological Q Wave (Deep, Wide)
  • Q wave >0.04 sec (1 small box) wide
  • Q wave >25% of R wave amplitude
  • Reduced or absent R wave progression
  • Irreversible — necrosis (permanent)
WINDOW THEORYDead tissue is electrically silent. Lead looks "through a window" at opposite wall forces going AWAY → pathological Q.
↑↓
Reciprocal Changes — The Mirror Effect

When one wall is injured (ST elevation), the lead viewing the opposite wall shows mirror-image ST depression. Reciprocal changes confirm true STEMI and indicate large culprit territory.

THE MIRROR

Hold a mirror up to a mountain (ST elevation) and you see a valley (ST depression). Reciprocal changes are the same electrical event viewed from opposite directions — greatly increases diagnostic confidence.

Inferior lead (II/III/aVF) — ST Elevation
Lateral lead (I/aVL) — Reciprocal ST Depression
⚡
Hyperacute T Waves — The EARLIEST Occlusion Sign

Before ST elevation develops, hyperacute T waves appear — tall, peaked, broad-based, symmetric. This may be the only ECG sign in the first minutes of coronary occlusion.

⚠ GRAUER PEARLHyperacute T waves + chest pain = possible occlusion MI even WITHOUT STEMI criteria. Repeat ECG every 5–10 min. Do NOT wait for ST elevation to activate cath lab!

MI Localization

Different coronary arteries supply different walls. Which leads are affected tells you exactly which artery is blocked.

NEIGHBORHOOD POWER OUTAGE

Coronary arteries are power lines for neighborhoods (heart walls). RCA goes down → south-facing houses (II, III, aVF) lose power → inferior STEMI. The ECG tells you WHICH power line is down by showing which neighborhood is dark.

RV
RV Infarction — Critical Management
  • Occurs with proximal RCA occlusion (inferior MI)
  • Diagnose: right-sided leads — ST elevation in V4R ≥1mm
  • Classic triad: hypotension + JVD + clear lung sounds
  • AVOID nitroglycerin — RV is preload-dependent
  • Give IV fluid 250–500 mL NS bolus to maintain preload
⚠ LIFE-THREATENING MISTAKENitrates in RV MI → profound hypotension → cardiac arrest. ALWAYS obtain right-sided leads with EVERY inferior STEMI. V4R elevation confirms RV involvement.
☠
The "Widow Maker" — Left Main / Proximal LAD

Left main coronary artery occlusion affects all left-sided territory simultaneously — highest mortality of all STEMIs.

ECG PATTERNST elevation in I, aVL, V1–V6 (extensive anterior) + ST depression in II, III, aVF. Cardiogenic shock common (>40% LV territory). High-degree AV block risk.
  • Emergent PCI — do NOT delay for any reason
  • RBBB + hemiblock may precede complete AV block
  • Consider IABP or Impella for hemodynamic support

Axis Determination

The average direction of ventricular depolarization. Deviations are clinical clues pointing to hidden pathology.

WEATHER VANE ON YOUR HEART

The cardiac axis is a weather vane showing which direction the electrical "wind" blows. Normally it points down-left (heart's natural lean). Obesity, hypertrophy, infarction, or fascicular blocks shift the vane — and that deviation is your clinical clue.

Only Leads I and aVF needed — perpendicular to each other, dividing frontal plane into 4 quadrants.

Left Axis Deviation (LAD)
  • Pathologic if more negative than −30°
  • Check Lead II: if QRS more negative than Lead I → pathologic LAD
  • Causes: LAHB (most common), inferior MI, LBBB, LVH, obesity

  • Causes: RBBB, RVH, pulmonary embolism, lateral wall MI, COPD
  • New RAD + sudden dyspnea + tachycardia → think PE!

◈ Module 08

Chamber Enlargement & LVH

More muscle mass generates more voltage. Hypertrophy leaves its signature in amplitude measurements.

SPEAKER VOLUME

Think of the heart as a speaker. A bigger woofer (more muscle) produces louder sound (greater voltage). LVH = massive subwoofer generating enormous QRS deflections — deep S in V1/V2 (signal going away) and tall R in V5/V6 (signal coming toward you).

LVH
Left Ventricular Hypertrophy Criteria
THE "35 AND 12" RULE (SOKOLOW-LYON)S depth in V1 or V2 + R height in V5 or V6 >35mm (if patient >35 years old)
OR R wave in aVL >12mm
  • Left axis deviation common
  • QRS at upper limits of normal (NOT wide)
  • Associated with Left Atrial Abnormality (P-Mitrale)
  • One criterion = sensitive; multiple = more specific
  • Age <35: threshold is >53mm total
⚠ CANNOT diagnose LVH if LBBB present!LBBB independently increases voltage, mimicking LVH. Also: LVH increases MI risk, sudden death risk, and ventricular dysrhythmia risk.
P
Atrial Abnormalities
P-Pulmonale — Right Atrial Abnormality
  • Peaked P waves >2.5mm in II, III, aVF ("peaked tent")
  • May see in V1–V2 as well
  • Causes: COPD, pulmonary HTN, RV failure, pulmonary stenosis
P-Mitrale — Left Atrial Abnormality
  • M-shaped (bifid) P waves in I, II, aVL
  • Duration >0.12 sec; deep negative terminal in V1
  • Causes: mitral stenosis, LHF, LAE
  • High atrial fibrillation risk!

ECG — Short PR + Delta Wave (WPW Pattern)

🩺 Wolff-Parkinson-White Syndrome

Short PR (<0.12s). Wide QRS with delta wave (slurred initial upstroke). Secondary ST-T changes.

SECRET UNDERGROUND TUNNEL

WPW = accessory pathway bypassing AV tollbooth. Signal arrives early via tunnel (short PR), spreads slowly through muscle (delta wave + wide QRS). Creates dangerous tachycardias.

⚠ CRITICAL WPW RULENEVER use AV nodal blockers (adenosine, beta-blockers, CCBs, digoxin) if WPW + atrial fibrillation — can cause V-Fib. Use PROCAINAMIDE or synchronized cardioversion instead.
